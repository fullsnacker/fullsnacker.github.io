There are a lot of free hello world tutorials out there for learning Javascript but after learning the basics I was looking for a way to level up. For the project that I was referring to in the above article, we were also using ionic to develop. So Ionic 4 was on the roster for the ramp up and obviously, I went straight to Udemy. ]]> https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2019/07/11/the-5-best-visual-studio-code-themes-of-2019/feed/ 6 How to set up three monitors on a MacBook Pro https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2019/06/14/how-to-set-up-three-monitors-on-a-macbook-pro/ https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2019/06/14/how-to-set-up-three-monitors-on-a-macbook-pro/#comments Fri, 14 Jun 2019 15:50:21 +0000 https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/?p=1578 Everybody loves more screen space, especially programmers. And in today’s work environment most development teams and coders work off laptops (usually Macs). So the real struggle is finding more of it. As soon as I sit down, I immediately look for an external monitor to plug into. If possible to connect to two monitors, then I am really in luck. But the white whale of laptop setups is connecting three monitors to one laptop. Today, I’m going to share how I did it!

Computer

First things first, your computer has got to be able to handle the number of pixels it’s going to be pushing. I’m letting you know right off the hop, a 13” MacBook Pro cannot handle three displays regardless of the resolution. Two is the max, but you can keep the laptop open so it like 2.5 monitors.

I got my triple monitor setup working with my 15”, 2.6 GHz Intel Core i7 processor, 16GB memory, Radeon Pro 450 2 GB Intel HD Graphics 530 1536 MB, MacBook Pro. If you would like to know everything I use in my setup, check out my article on it at https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/12/10-essentials-for-my-computer-setup/.

Dongle

One of the most important things about making the triple monitor setup work is the dongle. Now I’m going to save my rant on the dongle life for another time but you need to choose the right dongle. I recommend the iHaper USB-C hub because it’s the one I use and its worked great. If you go with another dongle/hub, make sure that it has at least one HDMI port (for monitor), one USB-C port (for power), and one standard USB port (for keyboard).

Cables

This is one that I think a lot of people don’t think of when they’re getting their setup wired. Since the MacBook Pro’s only have USB-C inputs you either have to use a converter to connect your HDMI monitor or, and this is the one I use, you buy HDMI to USB-C cables. You will also need just a regular HDMI cable as well that will plug into the dongle.

Monitors

Three monitors is the max that a 15” MacBook Pro can handle but only one can be 4k. The others need to be 1080p. You can, however, have the laptop open as another display. 13” MacBooks can only handle two external displays and they cannot be 4k. If you’re more interested in quality then, you can connect two 4k monitors to your 15” MacBook.

Laptop Stand

This is not necessary but if you’re like me and like to keep the desk clutter to a minimum, it’s a huge help. I use the Arc upright laptop stand. It keeps the cable chaos under control as well as looking pretty cool.

Connecting

  1. The laptop must be on or sleeping (must not be shutdown)
  2. Plug the regular HDMI cable into dongle and monitor
  3. Plug power cable into dongle and power source (When multiple displays are plugged in, the laptop must be plugged in to operate)
  4. Plug keyboard into the dongle (optional if you plan on using the laptop as a display)
  5. Connect HDMI-to-USB-C cables to monitors.
  6. Plug the dongle into one side of the laptop
  7. Connect the other two HDMI-to-USB-C cables to the laptop
  8. Click mouse or type on the keyboard to wake up the laptop
  9. Monitors should now all be displaying

Settings

To change the arrangement of the screens, go to System Preferences > Displays > Arrangement. You can now click and drag around how you would like your setup. A red border will signify the display that you are moving. You can also change the resolutions and other settings here to customize your setup.

Conclusion

I hope this has been helpful and if you are having any trouble getting it working, leave a comment below and I’ll do my best to help you out. Happy coding, designing, gaming or whatever you’re doing with your computer!

]]> https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/19/3-websites-to-use-when-designing-a-website/feed/ 2 7 Games I Created that Never Made it to the App Store https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/18/7-games-i-created-that-never-made-it-to-the-app-store/ https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/18/7-games-i-created-that-never-made-it-to-the-app-store/#respond Tue, 18 Dec 2018 17:35:20 +0000 https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/?p=1518

I’ve loved game development ever since I started programming. I ultimately found my career path in web development but I still really enjoy creating phone games. A couple made it to the app store but a many didn’t (I didn’t keep up with Apple’s app license so none are live now). So I thought I’d show the demos of all the games in the video above. All games were developed in ActionScript 3 and then compiled to iOS or Android through a tool called Air. All assets and development were done by yours truly.

Tiny Planet

Tiny Planet with a game I created as a final project when I was in college for interactive media development. The video above is the intro to the game. I created all the assets in 3D Studio Max and then exported them out as images and made sprites. 

The objective of the game is to keep the missiles from striking earth by blocking them with a barge type spaceship for as long as you can. Tapping on the right and left of the screen propels the ship with momentum around the earth. There are also power ups that help you survive longer. The glowing ball-type objects are force fields that will protect from a single missile hit. The boxes are EMPs. When you swipe up (on mobile) or click one in the corner, a pulse goes destroying all missiles on the screen.

This was one of my favourite games to develop because it used a bunch of different fields like, 3D animation, graphic design, programming etc.

Beaver Boy

The most Canadian game possible. You are a beaver wearing a toque that drinks maple syrup for energy to chew through trees. The power up is when you grab a maple leaf you get a birch canoe and paddle very fast with a hockey stick. It’s also always snowing. The only thing I missed was saying sorry when you lose and having a polar bear in there (maybe in Beaver Boy 2 lol). 

As a Canadian I thought it would be funny to make a game that played to all the stereotypes. You tap as fast as you can to chew through the oncoming trees that will push you back and out of frame. As you chew your energy syrup goes down so you need to jump up and grab some more. Every so often you can grab a maple leaf which will make you invincible for 10 seconds. The point of the game is to chew through as many trees as possible.

Toaster Boy

This one has the exact same favour as Flappy Bird, incredible frustrating but very addictive. 

The game is simple. Pop the toast out of the toaster and try to tap right when he enters the toaster again to bounce him out. Bounce him as many times as you can. The more you bounce him the higher he will go until he floats out into space. But believe me if you could get him into space you are not human.

This was fast little game that I would eventually love to get on the store. It was super fun to make and play.

Robo-Gorilla

This game was another final project for school. I designed all the piece of Robo-Gorilla in Adobe illustrator and then used a tool called Dragon Bones to animate him in the games.

I created a short animation in after effects to tell the story of Robo-Gorilla. Captured in the jungle and taken back to a lab, scientists weaponized him with guns and armour. Enraged when he woke up, Robo-Gorilla goes on a rampage to try and escape the facility.

There was a lot more I wanted to add to the game like different characters and power-ups but it was still a fun prototype.

Jungle Jim

I made this game on my reading week. Every single frame was hand drawn and coloured (time consuming) but I liked the effect.

The point of the game is to eat as many flies as you can but beware of snakes in the tree that will eat you. You can avoid the snakes by either going invisible or swiping down (on mobile) and going to the under side of the tree. I spent most of my time on the drawings so those features never go ironed out. Creating those animations was really fun though.

Jack Astro

This game was a partnership between my brother and I. I was down visiting him in Toronto during my reading week and we created this game, a squirrel flying through space collecting acorns, avoiding asteroids and blasting through everything will a NOS jet pack. Sounds about as nutty as my brother and I are. 

We created the assets together and I developed it. The point of the game was to survive as long as possible without getting hit by an asteroid. You would collect acorns which you could then use to buy better handle on your jet pack or more NOS slots.

I still think this game has great potential and it is for sure one of my personal favorites.

The Mail Room

This was a quick little game I made over a weekend. Different coloured letters fall from a shoot and you have to tap to rotate the funnel with the correct colour to catch the letter, a kind of sorting reaction-type game. Every so often a parcel falls and you have to swipe it out of the way (on mobile). 

The graphics were fun to design and I enjoyed getting a quick hit of game development out.

Well those are some of the games that I’ve developed that didn’t make it to the app store. I have more but I’ll show those to you all a different time. Hope you enjoyed and let me know what your favourite game in the comments.

]]> https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/18/7-games-i-created-that-never-made-it-to-the-app-store/feed/ 0 5 Fundamental Tips for Learning How to Code https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/17/5-fundemental-tips-for-learning-how-to-code/ https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/17/5-fundemental-tips-for-learning-how-to-code/#comments Mon, 17 Dec 2018 17:32:05 +0000 https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/?p=1502 Being in the field of development for several years, one of the most common questions I get asked is, “What’s your best advice for someone learning to program?”. So I’ve put together my top 5 pieces of advice that I found most helpful in my career in programming. Hopefully you find them helpful as well and if you have any other advice to add, please leave a comment!

What do you want to program?

This first one here might not sound like advice but it really is. Many people come into the field wanting to start programming but don’t realize the amount languages and directions you can take with code. I’ll tell you right now you cannot do them all, so picking a direction is crucial.

For me, I started my career as a graphic designer which quickly transition into web development. I had done some preliminary app development before that but found myself thoroughly enjoying web development more and more. So HTML, CSS, and Javascript were the languages to learn. After getting well versed in those I moved on to SASS, PHP and MySQL. 

If I had chosen app development my choice of programming languages would have been very different. I’m not going to go into all the languages related to each field but it is important to ask yourself that question and find the answer.

Just start

This is probably my favorite piece of advice on coding and life in general. Just start. Honestly, if you want to do something you have to start. I understand something you can’t just do right away, but start doing something to get you there. Take steps in the direction you want to go. Don’t tell me you really want to be a programmer. Start coding. There are piles of resources from learning and expanding your knowledge. I have an article on the top 3 resources on how to learn to code. 

The best way to learn something is to get your hands dirty with it.


If you want to start programming or want to learn a new framework or different language, just start. Download a text editor and start writing some code.

From the ground up

Someone asked me if they should start learning a library first rather than the base language because the library was easier. And as I always say, start from the ground up.

When I was first learning to program, I didn’t want to use ANY libraries or frameworks. I wanted to write every piece of code myself so I knew exactly what everything was doing. Looking back I realize that was a little zealous and unrealistic but I believe the underlying drive was good.

Knowing the basics and being able to code in the vanilla language is crucial in my opinion to becoming a better programmer. 

Trying to learn frameworks and libraries without starting with the base languages is like trying to build a house without a foundation.

Try things

One of the best ways to learn how to code or learn a new framework or library is through tutorials. The amount of free education in the field of programming is astounding and if you want to know my top recommendations for where to learn you can check out my article on the subject here.

Tutorials are amazing but just going through a tutorial is not enough. You need to mess around with it, try different things, make it do something else. This is the probably my number one tip for learning how to code. 

I can honestly say I learn so much more when I do variations of a tutorial than just leaving it at what was shown. I often find several related tutorials and then try and combine them to create what I want to create. Now, this stems back to my first point of knowing what you want to program.

For example, when I started learning the React framework I went through a tutorial that showed me the basics as well as a tutorial that showed me how to make API calls. I combined the two tutorials to create a simple web app that pulled my profile and feed data from Instagram. I learned so much more from trying to get that app to work than just going through the tutorial. Learning like this helps you to really understand what is going on rather than just following along.

Start Simple

In my example above, I started learning the React framework by building a super simple app that just pulled information from Instagram’s API. Understanding the basics is huge as I mentioned in my point about starting from the ground up. 

Biting off more than you can chew is something that I have and often am guilty of. It’s important when learning code not to get overwhelmed because, no matter what language you start with, it’s a lot of information to take in. 

Making small wins is crucial in a developer’s career because it’s those moments that will push you on through the next challenge. Getting “Hello World” to show up on a screen in rewarding!

You got to love it

This is an extra point but it’s one I love throwing in at the end. You have got to love it. Coding is hours and days and months and years of learning. It takes a tremendous amount of time to become fluent in coding and even longer to become an expert. All that to say that it is an incredibly rewarding field that allows you to create amazing, beautiful and useful things that you can share with the world.

]]> https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/17/5-fundemental-tips-for-learning-how-to-code/feed/ 2 Top 5 Essential Web Development Tools https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/15/my-top-5-essential-web-development-tools/ https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/2018/12/15/my-top-5-essential-web-development-tools/#comments Sat, 15 Dec 2018 17:10:26 +0000 https://fullsnackdeveloper.com/?p=1487

As all web developers do, I have a set of tools I use on every web development project.  So I thought I would share mine with you. These are tools that I can say with all honesty have greatly increased my efficiency. I should mention that I work primarily with Apple products so some of these tools may not be available for PC users. However, there are equilvalents out there.

Visual Studio Code

Obviously a code editor is an essential for any programming development stack but I tried a lot of the popular code editors and VS Code is my favourite by far. I started out on Sublime Text and used it all through college and the first two years of my web development career. Then I switch companies and they used WebStorm. I liked it a lot but it wasn’t free so when I left that company I started trying other editors. I used atom for a while but found it to be a little slow and clunky. Then there was a large movement to VS Code so I thought I’d see what all the fuss was about. And after using it for a week I knew why. The speed, the plugins and the built in terminal are just a couple of my favourite features. It’s now an essential tool in my development belt. 

Sass (Syntactically Awesome Style Sheets)

Now as a web developer I spent a lot of time writing CSS. When I was at an FITC (Future Innovations Technology Conference) in Toronto quite a few years ago I went to a talk on Sass and it changed my development stack forever. I’m not going to go into huge detail about all its features but the site says

Sass is the most mature, stable, and powerful professional grade CSS extension language in the world.

Sass is a CSS preprocessor, an extension of CSS that adds incredible functionality. Sass is compiled through a tool called Compass into CSS. The amount of time writing in Sass has saved me is incalculable.

There are other CSS preprocessors like LESS or Stylus but Sass is mine and most people’s choice.

Bourbon Neat

After integrating Sass into my development stack the question of what grid system should I use arose. I had been playing around with a few other grid systems but when I started tinkering with Neat I immediately knew this was the one. Bourbon Neat is 

A lightweight and flexible Sass grid

Its simple and doesn’t use any flex-box (flex-box wasn’t always stable) but still has incredible customization so it fits every project I build. It uses Sass mixins to set column widths and offsets so I never need to add loads of classes to my HTML. 

Thoughtbot is the development team that created Neat and they also have some other great tools to checkout like Bourbon and Bitters (there’s a theme, yes).

Codekit

This is the ultimate tool! Codekit‘s main purpose is to compile and compress languages down for faster, better development but it does so much more than that. Its browser refresh feature is one of my favorites. I make a change in my code and it automatically recompiles and refreshes my browser. I know that’s not revolutionary but it makes it so simple to get back into a project. Just drag your project in and within a couple clicks, you are set up with Sass, Compass, Bourbon Neat etc. 

Another great feature is that it can take multiple Sass or Javascript files and compile them into one compressed file. A great way to increase load times for your website. 

Codekit has piles of other features that I’ll let you discover for yourself. I will mention that this is a Mac-only product. If you know of something similar for PC I would love it if you mentioned it in the comments.

MAMP

MAMP is the final piece of the puzzle. According to the website

MAMP is a free, local server environment that can be installed under macOS and Windows with just a few clicks. 

MAMP turns your computer into a local server so that you can run and test PHP. 

Most of my clients want to run the content for their websites so natural I use WordPress as the CMS (content management system). This means that I need to be able to develop in PHP and run MySQL databases. And that’s exactly way MAMP allows me to do.

Now MAMP does have a paid version but I have never felt the need to pay for it when the free version does exactly what I need. From my understanding, the paid version is just a better interface but if you feel that the pro version is essential, let me know it the comments.

MAMP is a mac product but I know there are PC tools that do the same, such as LAMP.

Those are my top 5 essential web development tools. If you have other tools that you use on every project I would love to hear about them. Always down to add or replace tools with better ones. Let’s keep making our code DRYer and our development time shorter!

I love to longboard, record music, paint, craft, workout, code, design while running two businesses and still have a social life, so time management is something I had to figure out. These are my three tips for managing your time better.

Focus

Now I know this may seem a super general but give me a second to explain. There is a misconception that multitasking is the most effective way to get things done but that’s not the case. Your brain can only think about one thing at a time, the variable (how good at multitasking you are) is how fast your brain can flip between subjects. And it’s in these times of flipping back and forth when valuable time is lost.

I had a great interview with Mitch from CodingWithMitch and the subject of time management came up. He had a great analogy about focus. He said

Imagine you’re stacking Jenga blocks. You keep stacking them up higher and higher. Then someone comes along and smacks the whole tower down. And you start stacking the blocks back up again.

The blocks are your thought process. When you are undisturbed and focusing on one subject (in this case code) you can stack your thought blocks very high. But when someone interrupts and you have to flip between subjects, you are forced to start stacking all over again.

Obviously, it’s not realistic to think that you could never go undisturbed once you start working but setting blocks of time to be intentionally focused on one project or problem can be hugely beneficial for your productivity.

Removing Time Wasters

All my life I’ve been told that I have too much time on my hands. This always irked me a little because we all the the same amount of time on our hands. We all have 24 hours in a day.

We all have the same amount of time on our hands. Do things that don’t waste it.

But it got me to thinking about why people feel this way. What I found was that people (including myself) have things in there life that eat away at their time when they don’t notice, making them feel as though others have more time than they do.

The key is to remove these time wasters. For me it was watching endless Facebook videos. I found myself wasters hours just watching meaningless videos and feeling unsatisfied afterwards. I removed the Facebook app from my phone and found hours of my life back! 

Now I’m not saying you have to cut Netflix and social apps from your life but being more mindful of things that are prone to waste your time can help you make your time more effective. And this perfectly flows into my next and last tip. 

Change Your Mentality

Time management isn’t about finding more time to work it’s about finding a proper balance between working and resting. What I was finding was that time wasters were not helping me rest or work but merely distracting me from things that I really wanted to be doing. 

So how do determine if something is helping you rest or just waste your time. And this is where you need to change your mentality. The question to ask yourself is 

Does this add value to my life?

I doesn’t have to be work but does this help me relax, or am I investing in a relationship or myself. The answer to that question will help you determine if that’s something you need/want or whether it’s just a distraction. Sometimes an episode of New Girl is a great way to unwind from a crazy day.

The key is to evaluate the ways you spend your time and in doing so you will see the areas where you are productive and the ways you waste your time. Change your mentality in this way and you’ll see that you’ll become more productive with your time in work and in relaxation.
